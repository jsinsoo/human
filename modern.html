01양자역학Quantum Mechanics
Quantum mechanics describes the behavior of matter and light at atomic and subatomic scales. Unlike classical physics, it treats energy, angular momentum, and other quantities as quantized in many systems. The wave function contains probability amplitudes rather than a simple picture of where a particle definitely is. The Schrödinger equation predicts how the wave function changes with time. The Born rule connects the mathematical wave function with measurable probabilities. Superposition allows one quantum state to combine several classically distinct possibilities. Interference patterns reveal that quantum objects can behave as waves even when detected one by one. The uncertainty principle limits simultaneous precision for quantities such as position and momentum. Quantum tunneling explains phenomena such as alpha decay, scanning tunneling microscopes, and some electronic devices. Entanglement shows that separated systems can share a single quantum description. Spin is an intrinsic quantum property that produces magnetic behavior and selection rules. The Pauli exclusion principle explains the structure of the periodic table and the stability of matter. Bosons can occupy the same quantum state, leading to lasers and Bose-Einstein condensates. Quantum mechanics is essential for understanding chemical bonding and molecular structure. Semiconductors, LEDs, solar cells, and transistors all rely on quantum principles. Quantum measurement raises deep questions about reality, information, and observation. Different interpretations of quantum mechanics explain the same experiments in different conceptual languages. Quantum control now allows researchers to manipulate individual atoms, ions, photons, and superconducting circuits. Quantum mechanics has been tested with extraordinary precision in countless experiments. Its central lesson is that microscopic reality follows rules very different from everyday intuition.

02상대성이론Relativity
Relativity reshapes the concepts of space, time, motion, and gravity. Special relativity begins from the principle that the laws of physics are the same in all inertial frames. It also states that the speed of light in vacuum is the same for every inertial observer. Time dilation means that moving clocks can run slower relative to an observer. Length contraction means that moving objects can be measured shorter along the direction of motion. Relativity of simultaneity shows that different observers may disagree about whether two events occur at the same time. Mass-energy equivalence reveals that mass is a concentrated form of energy. General relativity describes gravity as the curvature of spacetime rather than a simple force. Objects in free fall follow geodesics, the natural paths in curved spacetime. Massive objects bend light, producing gravitational lensing. Clocks closer to a massive body run differently from clocks farther away. Black holes are solutions of general relativity where spacetime curvature becomes extreme. Gravitational waves are ripples in spacetime generated by accelerating masses. Relativity correctly explains the anomalous precession of Mercury's orbit. GPS technology requires relativistic corrections for both speed and gravity. Relativity connects geometry with physical measurement. It is essential for cosmology, black hole physics, and high-energy astrophysics. The theory preserves objective laws while making measurements frame-dependent. Relativity and quantum mechanics together define the central tension of modern theoretical physics. Its greatest insight is that space and time are dynamic parts of the universe.

03양자장론Quantum Field Theory
Quantum field theory is the framework that merges quantum mechanics with special relativity. It treats particles as excitations of fields that exist throughout spacetime. An electron is not merely a tiny ball but a quantum excitation of the electron field. Particle creation and annihilation are natural processes in this framework. Feynman diagrams represent possible interaction processes between quantum fields. Quantum electrodynamics explains interactions between charged particles and photons. Quantum chromodynamics explains how quarks and gluons produce the strong interaction. The electroweak theory unifies electromagnetic and weak interactions at high energies. Gauge symmetry determines the structure of many fundamental interactions. The Higgs field gives mass to many elementary particles through symmetry breaking. Renormalization connects calculations at different energy scales with measurable results. Vacuum fluctuations show that empty space is not physically empty. The Casimir effect is one observable consequence of quantum fields in the vacuum. QFT explains scattering experiments in high-energy particle accelerators. Effective field theories allow physicists to describe phenomena without knowing every deeper detail. Condensed matter physics also uses field theory to describe collective excitations. QFT is mathematically demanding because it involves infinitely many degrees of freedom. It provides some of the most precise numerical predictions ever confirmed. It suggests that fields, not particles alone, are the deeper physical entities. The theory is central to the Standard Model and to many searches for new physics.
